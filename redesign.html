Project YouTube Redesign
Type Interface Redesign
Year 2025
Role UI/UX Designer
/ Overview (01)

The world's largest video platform — redesigned to actually respect the people watching it.

YouTube's interface has accumulated years of competing priorities — monetisation, recommendations, shorts, ads — layered on top of each other without a coherent visual system underneath. This conceptual redesign audits the core viewing experience and rebuilds it around one principle: the video should always win. The project covers the homepage, watch page, and search results — the three screens where friction is highest and attention is most fragile.

/ Design Decisions (02)

Card 1 mockup
01

Visual Hierarchy Collapse

Thumbnails, ads, and Shorts all compete at the same visual weight.

The homepage treats every piece of content identically — a 3-minute vlog, a 2-hour documentary, and a sponsored post all share the same card size, the same thumbnail ratio, and the same label treatment. The eye has nowhere to land.

A tiered card system — large editorial cards for long-form content, compact rows for shorts, and a clear visual separator for promoted content. Hierarchy is restored without removing any content type.
Card 2 mockup
02

Watch Page Clutter

The video is the smallest thing on the watch page.

On a typical 1080p screen, the video player competes with a comment section, an autoplay queue, channel info, action buttons, a description accordion, and a banner ad — all visible above the fold simultaneously.

A focused watch mode collapses secondary elements until the user signals intent — comments expand on scroll, the queue slides in on hover. The player takes 70% of the viewport by default.
Card 3 mockup
03

Search Result Noise

Search returns a mix of videos, ads, playlists, and Shorts with no clear distinction.

A query for a tutorial returns paid promotions, algorithmically recommended tangents, and unrelated Shorts in the same list as the actual result — with no visual or structural way to tell them apart at a glance.

Search results are grouped by content type with a sticky filter bar: Videos, Playlists, Channels, Shorts. Promoted content is labelled clearly and visually offset — present but not disguised.
Card 4 mockup
04

Dark Mode Inconsistency

Dark mode is surface-level — just grey boxes, not a real dark system.

YouTube's dark mode swaps white backgrounds for mid-grey (#212121) but leaves the elevation model, shadow system, and component contrast ratios largely unchanged. It feels like a filter, not a designed mode.

A true dark system built with layered surfaces — near-black base, elevated cards at distinct tonal steps, and reduced-saturation accents. Thumbnails remain the brightest elements in the viewport by design.
Card 5 mockup
05

Thumbnail Metadata Overload

Every card carries five lines of metadata before you've decided to watch.

Title (two lines), channel name, view count, upload date, and duration badge — all stacked below every thumbnail. On a grid of 12 cards, that's 60 lines of text competing for attention before a single decision is made.

Progressive disclosure on cards — thumbnail and title only at rest. Channel, date, and view count appear on hover as a subtle overlay. Duration badge stays visible always as it affects watch intent directly.
Card 6 mockup
06

Navigation Sprawl

The sidebar has 22 items — and none of them feel primary.

Home, Shorts, Subscriptions, Library, History, Watch Later, trending, music, gaming, news, sports, fashion — all at the same visual weight in a single scrollable column. The result is a nav that communicates nothing about what matters.

A collapsed sidebar with four primary destinations — Home, Subscriptions, Library, Explore. Everything else lives under Explore as a contextual grid. The sidebar expands on intent, not by default.
